# Good Math

## Key Concepts

This book explores fascinating mathematical concepts from number theory, logic, and computer science, presenting rigorous ideas through accessible explanations that reveal the beauty and power of mathematical thinking. Core topics include understanding different number systems beyond familiar integers including irrational, transcendental, and transfinite numbers, exploring formal logic and proof techniques that underpin computer science, and examining how mathematical structures like groups, graphs, and automata model computational processes. The content covers both pure mathematics showing elegant proofs and surprising results, and applied mathematics demonstrating how abstract concepts solve practical problems in programming, cryptography, and algorithm design. The book emphasizes that mathematical reasoning provides tools for precise thinking applicable far beyond calculation, teaching readers to construct rigorous arguments and identify flaws in seemingly plausible but incorrect reasoning.

## Who Should Read It and Why

This book targets programmers curious about the mathematical foundations underlying computer science who want deeper understanding than typical CS curriculum provides, along with math enthusiasts seeking engaging explanations of advanced topics without requiring graduate-level prerequisites. Developers who implement algorithms without understanding their mathematical basis will discover why certain approaches work, learning the theory that explains empirical observations about performance and correctness. The content particularly benefits those intimidated by mathematical notation who gave up on formal education but retain intellectual curiosity, finding that clear prose explanations make previously inaccessible ideas comprehensible. Software engineers encountering concepts like big-O notation, regular expressions, or cryptographic primitives in daily work will appreciate seeing the mathematical principles that make these practical tools work, connecting abstract theory to familiar programming tasks.

## Practical Applications

Readers will learn to understand different infinities through Cantor's diagonal argument showing some infinite sets are larger than others, apply proof by induction for verifying recursive algorithms, and use modular arithmetic principles that underpin cryptographic systems like RSA. The book demonstrates working with number theory concepts including prime factorization and greatest common divisors essential for cryptography, understanding formal grammars and automata theory that formalize language processing, and applying graph theory for modeling networks and shortest path algorithms. Practical examples cover using set theory for database queries and data structure design, applying logic and proofs for verifying program correctness, and understanding computational complexity theory for reasoning about algorithm efficiency.
